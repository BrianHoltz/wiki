# Net Beneficiaries of Government Redistribution and Market Distortions
## Demographic and Occupational Breakdown

**Date:** March 2026  
**Summary estimate:** ~65% of U.S. residents (≈215 million people) live in households that are net beneficiaries of the aggregate government intervention apparatus when you account for direct fiscal transfers, government employment premia, occupational licensing rents, industry subsidies, and other market distortions.

## 1. Methodology and Framing

"Net beneficiary" means the household receives more in government-allocated value — cash transfers, in-kind benefits, above-market compensation, protected rents, or legally suppressed competition — than it pays in taxes (federal, state, local combined). This is a *stock-and-flow* estimate based on current-year cashflows, not lifetime actuarial calculations.

Primary sources: CBO distributional analyses; Bureau of Labor Statistics occupational data; Census Bureau program participation surveys (ACS, CPS); academic literature on licensing rents (Kleiner, Krueger), zoning costs (Glaeser, Gyourko), and public choice theory (Tullock, Buchanan, Stigler).

| Program | Recipients | Net-beneficiary households (est.) |
|---|---|---|

### 2.3 Education Subsidies

| Category | Scale |
|---|---|
| Pell Grants | ~7M students annually |
| Subsidized federal student loans | ~43M borrowers outstanding |
| Universal public K-12 | ~49M students (direct in-kind) |
| Pre-K / Head Start | ~800K children |

**Key point on K-12:** Although every household with school-age children receives in-kind public education, the *net* question is whether property-tax payments cover the cost. For lower-income households they do not — the subsidy is large and real. For high-income households in well-funded districts, it's closer to a wash or net payer.

**Fiscal-transfer-only net beneficiaries:** CBO's income quintile analyses suggest roughly 60% of households receive more in federal transfers and in-kind benefits than they pay in federal taxes. Adding state/local (net of state/local taxes) does not materially change this number; state/local transfers (Medicaid admin, schools, pensions) roughly mirror state/local taxes by income quintile.

**Fiscal-only estimate: ~55–60% of households (~72–78M households, ~185–200M people).**

## 3. Government Employment Premia

Government employment carries a measurable total-compensation premium over comparable private-sector work — estimates range from 10–40% depending on methodology, sector, and level of government, driven primarily by defined-benefit pensions, job security, and health benefits.

| Sector | Employees | Avg. comp premium | Net-beneficiary households (est.) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Federal civilian | 3.0M | ~15–25% | ~2.5M (those not already in transfer category) |
| State government | 5.5M | ~10–20% | ~4M additional |
| Local government (non-education) | 7.5M | ~10–15% | ~5M additional |
| K-12 teachers/administrators | 7.5M | ~8–20% (via defined-benefit pensions) | ~4M additional |
| Higher education (public) | 2.5M | varies | ~1.5M additional |
| Active duty military | 1.3M | below-market cash but large in-kind (housing, healthcare, education) | ~1M |

**Estimate: ~18M additional net-beneficiary households from government employment premia not already captured above.**

## 4. Occupational Licensing Rents

Licensing laws restrict entry, suppress supply, and transfer rents from consumers (and unlicensed would-be workers) to incumbents. Economist Morris Kleiner estimates licensing raises incumbent wages 10–18% on average, with healthcare licensing producing the largest absolute rents.

| Occupation | Licensed workers (est.) | Licensing rent mechanism | Net-beneficiary households |
|---|---|---|---|
| Physicians (MD/DO) | 1.1M | AMA-lobbied supply caps via residency slots (Medicare-funded), restricted scope of practice | ~900K |
| Dentists | 210K | Prevents dental therapists, hygienist independence | ~190K |
| Nurses (RN/LPN/NP) | 4.3M | Scope of practice limits on NPs in many states | Mixed — NPs hurt, RNs slightly helped |
| Pharmacists | 320K | Prevents tech substitution; mail-order restrictions | ~280K |
| Lawyers | 1.3M | Bar exam cartel, UPL laws block paraprofessionals | ~1.0M additional (many are net payers due to income taxes, but licensing adds $100K+/yr) |
| Electricians / plumbers / HVAC | 2.8M | Journeyman/master licensing limits entry | ~2M |
| Cosmetologists / barbers | 760K | ~1,500 hours of training for a haircut; captures new entrants | ~600K |
| Real estate agents | 1.5M | NAR-lobbied commission structures, state licensing | ~1.0M |
| Financial advisors / brokers | 500K | FINRA/SEC credentialing creates moats | ~300K |
| Contractors / general builders | 1.5M | State licensing with experience requirements | ~1.0M |
| Other licensed occupations (teachers, CPAs, engineers, etc.) | ~8M | Varies | ~4M |

**Critical caveat:** Many licensed professionals (especially physicians and lawyers) earn high incomes and therefore pay high taxes. Their licensing rent may not make them net beneficiaries on fiscal flows alone. But the economic rent *from licensing* is real value extracted from consumers and unlicensed competitors — they are net beneficiaries *of that specific distortion* even if net fiscal payers overall. The question asked, correctly, to count all distortions, not just fiscal ones.

**Estimate: ~10–12M additional households are net beneficiaries principally because of licensing rents not already captured.**

## 6. Land and Real Estate Distortions

Zoning, land use restrictions, and historic preservation rules create artificial scarcity in high-demand markets, inflating home values for incumbents and pricing out renters and new buyers.

| Category | Scale | Net effect |
|---|---|---|
| Homeowners in supply-constrained metros (NY, SF, Boston, LA, DC, Seattle, Chicago) | ~20–30M people in homeowning households | Net beneficiaries of zoning scarcity |
| Homeowners in non-supply-constrained markets | ~80–90M people | More ambiguous — price inflation less from zoning |
| Renters everywhere | ~115M people in renter households | Net payers of housing distortions |
| Prop 13 / assessment cap beneficiaries (California specifically) | ~6M homeowning households in CA | Clear subsidy from future buyers and renters |

**Important nuance:** Homeowners benefit from home value appreciation driven by zoning, but they also pay higher prices when they bought in (unless they owned before the restriction tightened). Long-tenured owners in supply-constrained metros are clear winners. Recent buyers are mixed. Renters are clear losers.

**Estimate: ~15–20M additional net-beneficiary households from land-use distortions (long-tenured owners in constrained markets not already counted).**

## 10. Political Economy Interpretation

This is not an accident. It is the equilibrium. Public choice theory (Buchanan, Tullock) predicts exactly this: concentrated benefits and diffuse costs produce durable majority coalitions that sustain and expand the transfer and regulatory state regardless of aggregate welfare effects.

The key insight is that the *beneficiary coalition is not monolithic* — it is heterogeneous and often incoherent. Retired homeowners, licensed dentists, defense engineers, food stamp recipients, and municipal union members don't organize together or even consciously identify as "beneficiaries." But their diffuse political votes and organized lobbying converge on the same policy outcome: protect existing transfers and rents.

**The anti-reform coalitions are strong precisely because:**
1. Benefits are concentrated and visible (doctors know licensing protects them; seniors know Medicare exists)
2. Costs are diffuse and invisible (no one knows their reduced wages or higher prices due to market distortions)
3. Net payers in the top 30–35% are internally divided (high-income licensed professionals benefit from some distortions they would otherwise fight)
4. Reform threatens loss aversion more than it promises gain

**The ~35% net payers** are concentrated in the top two quintiles, in unprotected private-sector employment, in the entrepreneurial class, and in regions with high tax burdens and fewer government transfers. They are the ones who feel the fiscal and economic drag most acutely — and who constitute the primary constituency for reform.

## 11. Net Producers, Financers, and Victims

This section reverses the lens. The question is: **who, in the current year, is actively financing the transfers, subsidizing the distortions, and bearing the deadweight losses?** This counts only *current* net contributors — not retirees who formerly paid in, not children who will pay in later, only people generating and delivering net value to the transfer apparatus right now.

The three categories of net payer:
- **Fiscal payers**: pay more in taxes than they receive in direct transfers and in-kind benefits
- **Rent victims**: pay above-market prices for goods and services (or earn below-market wages) because entry is restricted for others' benefit
- **Regulatory/distortion losers**: suppressed competitors, displaced workers, consumers taxed by protection, entrepreneurs blocked by incumbents

These categories overlap substantially. A self-employed software engineer in Austin pays high federal income taxes (fiscal payer), overpays for dental work due to licensing cartels (rent victim), and competes in a market where capital allocation is distorted by IP rent-seeking (distortion loser). He is simultaneously all three.

| Top 1% (income >~$600K) | ~40% | ~25% | 1% |

**Adding state and local taxes** (which are proportionally more regressive via sales and property taxes) compresses this somewhat, but does not change the dominant story: the top 20–25% of the income distribution is carrying the overwhelming majority of the fiscal load.

**Current-year fiscal net payers — individual estimate:**

| Category | Est. individuals | Notes |

### 11.3 Victims of Specific Distortions (Beyond Fiscal Flows)

Fiscal accounting understates the true cost. The deadweight losses from market distortions are borne mostly by people who don't show up as "net fiscal payers" — they're paying above-market prices, earning below-market wages, or operating in artificially shrunk markets.

#### 11.3.1 Licensing Victims (Excluded Competitors and Overcharged Consumers)

| Distortion | Victims | Estimated annual cost born per household |
|---|---|---|
| Medical/dental licensing (suppressed supply, restricted scope of practice) | ~335M U.S. residents as healthcare consumers | ~$2,000–4,000/yr per household in higher costs than competitive market would produce (Kleiner estimates) |
| Legal licensing (UPL laws block paralegal services, online services, non-lawyer dispute resolution) | ~200M Americans who ever need legal services | ~$500–2,500/yr in unnecessary legal costs |
| Cosmetology licensing (1,000+ hour requirements benefit incumbents) | ~50M annual salon consumers | Modest per-household, but real: ~$100–300/yr |
| Occupational licensing in childcare (pushes up costs, restricts supply) | ~32M families with children in care | ~$2,000–5,000/yr in higher childcare costs |
| Real estate licensing / NAR practices | ~5M home buyers/sellers annually | ~$10,000–30,000 per transaction in commissions above competitive market rate |

**Active licensing victims in any given year:** essentially the entire consuming public for healthcare, legal, and childcare — ~250M people in their consumer role — with the typical middle-class household losing $5,000–10,000/year in aggregate to licensing rents across all categories.

#### 11.3.2 Trade Protection Victims

| Protection | Domestic consumers harmed | Annual cost |
|---|---|---|
| Steel / aluminum tariffs | All manufacturers using steel/aluminum as inputs; all consumers of downstream goods | ~$9B/year total; ~$70 per household |
| Sugar program | All consumers of sugar and sugar-containing foods | ~$3B/year; ~$23 per household |
| Jones Act | Residents of Hawaii, Alaska, Puerto Rico, Guam most acutely; all coastal shipping users | ~$1.8B/year; highly concentrated in island states: ~$1,500–2,500/yr per Hawaiian household |
| Auto / CAFE / content rules | All car buyers | Embedded in vehicle pricing; ~$2,000–5,000 per new vehicle |
| Textile / apparel duties | Lower-income consumers (spend higher % of income on clothing) | Regressive: costs bottom quintile ~2x as % of income as top quintile |
| Solar tariffs | Anyone seeking to install solar; utilities buying panels | ~$0.5–1B/year; concentrated in early-adopter markets |

**Active trade-protection victims:** essentially all 130M U.S. households as consumers; lower-income households bear a proportionately higher burden due to regressivity of goods price inflation.

#### 11.3.3 Zoning and Housing Distortion Victims

| Category | Population | Annual cost |
|---|---|---|
| Renters in supply-constrained metros (NYC, SF, LA, Boston, DC, Seattle, Miami) | ~30–40M people in high-cost rental markets | Median rent premium vs. competitive supply: $6,000–18,000/yr per household (Glaeser/Gyourko estimates) |
| First-time home buyers priced out of constrained markets | ~2–3M annual buyers | Higher purchase prices attributable to supply restriction: $100,000–500,000 in lifetime wealth effect |
| Workers unable to move to high-productivity cities due to housing costs | ~2–5M workers/year (Hsieh-Moretti estimate of misallocation) | GDP loss of ~$1.4T/year (aggregate); individual income loss ~$8,700/yr per misallocated worker |
| Small landlords competing against distortion-entrenched incumbents | ~7M small landlords | Mixed; many benefit from same supply restriction |

**Active housing-distortion victims in the current year:** ~35–45M people as renters in supply-constrained markets, plus a diffuse national productivity loss from labor misallocation.

### 11.4 Composite Profile: The Net Producer / Net Victim

Synthesizing across fiscal, licensing, trade, and land-use distortions:

**The "pure" net producer** — someone currently paying in on all dimensions and extracting rents on none — is disproportionately:

- **Age:** 28–55 (post-school, pre-Medicare/SS collection)
- **Income:** Top 20–40% by household income (~$85K–$250K range) — high enough to be a clear fiscal net payer, not high enough to have escaped consumer markets where licensing rents hit hardest
- **Employment:** Private-sector, non-government, non-licensed, non-protected-industry
- **Geography:** High-cost metro (high taxes, housing-distortion victim as renter or recent buyer), or competitive market city (fiscal payer, trade victim)
- **Sector:** Technology, private-sector engineering, unprotected finance, logistics, retail management, construction (non-union, non-licensed)
- **Tenure:** Not a long-tenured homeowner in a supply-constrained market (those benefit from zoning rents)
- **Household structure:** Working-age adults without significant dependent benefits, no government employment in household

**This profile describes roughly 70–90 million current individuals** (~21–27% of the U.S. population), with meaningful variation in how many distortion categories they are net victims of simultaneously.

### 11.5 Aggregate Quantification

| Dimension | Net payer / victim count | Magnitude per person/yr |
|---|---|---|
| Fiscal net payers (federal + state/local) | ~75–85M individuals | −$12,000 to −$200,000+ depending on income |
| Licensing rent victims (consumer role) | ~250M individuals | −$3,000 to −$10,000 aggregate across categories |
| Trade protection victims | ~130M households (~320M people) | −$200 to −$2,500 depending on consumption basket |
| Housing distortion victims (renters in constrained markets) | ~35–45M people | −$6,000 to −$18,000/yr |
| Labor misallocation (workers unable to move to higher-productivity city) | ~5–10M workers | −$5,000 to −$15,000/yr in suppressed wages |

**The non-overlapping count of individuals who are net losers on at least one dimension in the current year: essentially everyone who is not a net fiscal beneficiary** — somewhere between 90–110 million people bear a meaningful net burden annually. The top 10–15% of earners are the largest absolute payers; lower-middle-income renters and consumers are the most proportionally squeezed.

**The irony:** many of the 65% net-beneficiaries of fiscal redistribution are simultaneously victims of the regulatory apparatus — a Medicaid recipient who overpays for healthcare because licensing restricts supply, or a food-stamp recipient in Hawaii who pays above-market food costs due to the Jones Act. The fiscal benefit and the market-distortion harm often hit the same household, and the net may be smaller than the gross flows suggest.

---

### 11.6 What It Costs Each Net Producer to Fund Each Net Beneficiary

At the central estimates:
- ~90–110M current net contributors supporting ~205–230M net beneficiaries
- Fiscal burden alone: federal net transfer per net-payer household ≈ $25,000–35,000/year
- Adding regulatory rent flows (licensing, trade, zoning) to the fiscal burden: total annual wealth transfer from net producers to net beneficiaries estimated at **$6–8 trillion** (fiscal transfers ~$4.5T; rent transfers ~$1.5–2T; housing distortion wealth transfers ~$0.5–1T)
- Per net-contributing household: **$60,000–100,000/year** in total transfers financed and distortions borne

This is the equilibrium the majority coalition votes to preserve.
